若
Uncategorized
Kun: wakai, moshi, moshikuwa, gotoshi  |  On: jaku, nyaku
young ・ if ・ or ・ as if ・ to follow ・ you (archaic)

Explanation

Shirakawa understands 若 as a pictograph of a female shaman: her long hair streaming, both hands raised as she dances and prays to solicit a divine oracle. A ritual “sai” vessel was added—into which prayer texts were later placed—signaling formal chanting and petition; in the modern form, the once upraised hands have been simplified into the grass-like crown at the top. The character thus shows a medium possessed by the deity and entrusted to relay the heavenly will, which underlies uses where 若 expresses “as is/as if,” the faithful conveying of a message, and by extension the idea of “following” that will. Because such deity-servants were young, the graph also came to mean “young.” In classical texts it can substitute for 女, 如, or 而 and serves for meanings such as “if,” “or,” and even the second-person “you.” The related 如, built from 女 with a chanting sign, depicts the same praying posture and closely parallels 若 in structure.
